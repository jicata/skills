Separation of concerns · why two agents

Two agents, one PR: the coder / reviewer split

The orchestrator and the loop are shared — but the two agents inside it are deliberately not the same. They run different skills, load different lenses, and chase different goals. They only ever meet at the PR. That separation is what makes an autonomous review trustworthy: the thing grading the code isn't the thing that wrote it.

Why split them at all

Three reasons the review is a separate agent rather than the coder grading its own work.

Independent context
The reviewer is spun up cold, fresh per PR. It never saw the coder's reasoning, so it can't inherit the coder's blind spots or be talked out of a finding by the mind that made the mistake.
Different lenses, wider net
The coder optimizes for building the slice (vsa, dotnet, deep modules) and grades one axis; the reviewer grades three — requirements, standards, and CI — through its own persona + smell baseline. Different doctrine surfaces different failure modes.
The split is free
Both run on the same cheap workhorse model. Independent review costs nothing but a second agent — so the pattern uses one every single round instead of trusting self-review.
Where it's shared vs split: the coder and reviewer diverge exactly where judgement should be independent — the skills they run, the work they do (the coder builds one axis; the reviewer grades three), and the lens they load. They converge on everything that must be common ground — the orchestrator above, the shared tdd / guardrail / doc doctrine, and the repo's profile + resilience below. Same machine, same model, two minds. That's the whole reason an autonomous review is worth trusting.
